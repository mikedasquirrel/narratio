Gender Effect Analysis

Testing Jung et al. (2014) Finding

Original Study: Feminine-named hurricanes ‚Üí 2x more deaths

Hypothesis: Feminine names perceived as less dangerous ‚Üí Less evacuation ‚Üí More deaths

Our Analysis (246 Landfall Storms, 1979-2023):

  • Male-named hurricanes: 14.4 average deaths
  • Female-named hurricanes: 14.4 average deaths
  • Ratio: 1.00x (equal)
  • Statistical significance: p = 0.99 (not significant)

Regression Finding: Female gender coefficient = +0.279 (MORE deaths when controlling for storm strength)

Direction aligns with Jung et al. but effect is modest in our sample.

Phonetic Harshness Effect (NEW)

Novel Hypothesis: Harsh-Sounding Names ‚Üí Better Response

Theory: Names with harsh sounds (Katrina, Kyle) perceived as more dangerous than soft sounds (Sandy, Laura)

Our Analysis:

  • Harsh-named hurricanes (K, T, D): 12.2 average deaths
  • Soft-named hurricanes (S, L, M, N): 17.4 average deaths
  • Direction: Harsh < Soft (supports hypothesis)
  • Correlation: -0.084
  • Statistical significance: p = 0.28 (not significant, but trending)

Regression Finding: Harshness coefficient = -0.288 (FEWER deaths for harsh names)

Novel contribution: Phonetic characteristics may affect risk perception beyond gender.

Policy Implications

Recommendations for NOAA/National Hurricane Center

  1. Continue Alternating Male/Female Names: Implemented in 1979, important for reducing gender bias
  2. Consider Phonetic Harshness Distribution: Trend toward harsh names (K, T sounds) may improve threat perception
  3. Public Education: Educate about name bias - "Don't judge a hurricane by its name"
  4. Media Guidelines: Encourage media to emphasize storm characteristics over name
  5. Warning System Enhancement: Visual warnings (categories, wind speeds) reduce reliance on name perception

Impact: Even small improvements in evacuation rates could save dozens of lives per major hurricane.

What This Reveals

Nominative Determinism in Survival

Hurricanes provide the ultimate test of nominative effects because stakes are life and death.

The Mechanism:

  1. Storm assigned a name (alphabetically by year)
  2. Name has characteristics (gender, phonetics, memorability)
  3. People perceive storm threat based partly on name
  4. Perception affects evacuation decisions
  5. Decisions affect survival outcomes
