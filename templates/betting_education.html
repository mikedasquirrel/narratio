core betting concepts

American Odds (Moneyline)
American odds use positive (+) and negative (-) numbers to indicate underdogs and favorites. Negative odds show how much you need to bet to win $100. Positive odds show how much you win on a $100 bet.
Example: Boston Bruins -150 vs Montreal Canadiens +130
‚Ä¢ Bet $150 on Bruins to win $100 (favorite)
‚Ä¢ Bet $100 on Canadiens to win $130 (underdog)
‚Ä¢ Implied probability: Bruins 60%, Canadiens 43.5%
Implied Probability
The percentage chance of an outcome according to the odds. This represents the market's assessment of likelihood plus the bookmaker's margin (vig). Understanding implied probability is crucial for finding value bets.
Odds Type Formula Example Result
Negative (-) |odds| / (|odds| + 100) -150 60.0%
Positive (+) 100 / (odds + 100) +130 43.5%
üí° Pro Tip: Total implied probability often exceeds 100% due to the bookmaker's margin. In our example: 60% + 43.5% = 103.5%. The 3.5% excess is the vig.
Expected Value (EV)
Expected Value measures the average return of a bet if placed many times. Positive EV (+EV) bets are profitable long-term, while negative EV (-EV) bets lose money over time. Professional bettors only place +EV bets.
EV = (Win Probability √ó Win Amount) - (Loss Probability √ó Loss Amount)

Example: Our model shows 72.8% win probability on a -120 bet
‚Ä¢ Win amount: $83.33 (on $100 bet)
‚Ä¢ EV = (0.728 √ó $83.33) - (0.272 √ó $100)
‚Ä¢ EV = $60.66 - $27.20 = +$33.46
‚Ä¢ ROI = 33.46% (highly positive EV)

advanced betting concepts

Kelly Criterion
Mathematical formula for optimal bet sizing based on edge and bankroll. Full Kelly can be aggressive; most professionals use fractional Kelly (25-50%) for risk management.
Kelly % = (bp - q) / b
Where: b = decimal odds - 1, p = win probability, q = loss probability

Example: 72.8% win rate on -120 odds
‚Ä¢ b = 0.833 (decimal odds 1.833 - 1)
‚Ä¢ p = 0.728, q = 0.272
‚Ä¢ Kelly % = (0.833 √ó 0.728 - 0.272) / 0.833 = 40.3%
‚Ä¢ Quarter Kelly = 10.1% of bankroll (recommended)
‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Never bet full Kelly. Even with accurate probabilities, variance can cause significant drawdowns. Professional bettors typically use 10-25% of Kelly.
Closing Line Value (CLV)
The difference between the odds when you bet and the closing odds (just before game start). Consistently beating closing lines indicates skill and is predictive of long-term profitability.
Example: You bet Bruins -150 at 2pm
Closing line: Bruins -170 at 7pm
CLV = Getting -150 when market closed at -170
Result: Positive CLV (you got better odds than closing)
üí° Fact: Bettors who consistently beat closing lines by 2.5% or more are virtually guaranteed to be profitable long-term, regardless of short-term results.

essential betting terminology

Bankroll
Total funds allocated for betting. Should be money you can afford to lose. Professional bankroll management never risks more than 1-5% per bet.
Unit
Standard bet size, typically 1% of bankroll. Betting in units allows for consistent staking regardless of bankroll size. Our system uses 1-2 unit bets.
Juice/Vig/Vigorish
Bookmaker's commission built into odds. Standard is -110 on both sides (4.5% vig). Lower juice means better value for bettors.
Sharp Money
Bets from professional/winning bettors. Books adjust lines based on sharp action. Following sharp money is often profitable.
Steam Move
Sudden, simultaneous line movement across multiple sportsbooks, usually indicating sharp action or significant news.
Middle/Middling
Betting both sides at different lines to potentially win both bets if result lands in the "middle". Requires significant line movement.
Hedge
Placing a bet on the opposite outcome to guarantee profit or minimize loss. Often done with futures or parlays nearing completion.
Push
Tie against the spread or total, resulting in bet refund. Cannot happen with moneyline bets in NHL (overtime/shootout determines winner).

risk management principles

Variance and Standard Deviation
Even with a 72.8% win rate, losing streaks are inevitable due to variance. Understanding and accepting variance is crucial for long-term success. Our system's standard deviation is 18.3% monthly.

Maximum Drawdown Management
Drawdown is the peak-to-trough decline in bankroll. Even profitable systems experience drawdowns. Our historical max drawdown is 8.2%, but be prepared for up to 20% in extreme cases.
Drawdown Rules:
‚Ä¢ 10% drawdown: Reduce bet sizes by 25%
‚Ä¢ 15% drawdown: Reduce bet sizes by 50%
‚Ä¢ 20% drawdown: Pause betting, reassess model
‚Ä¢ Always maintain 100+ unit bankroll for variance

common betting mistakes to avoid

‚ùå Chasing Losses: Increasing bet sizes after losses to "get even" quickly. This violates bankroll management and often leads to bankruptcy.
‚ùå Parlaying for Value: Combining multiple bets reduces probability multiplicatively. A 3-team parlay of 70% bets only wins 34.3% of the time.
‚ùå Betting Subjectively: Letting team loyalty or gut feelings override model predictions. Emotional betting is the fastest way to lose money.
‚ùå Poor Record Keeping: Not tracking bets makes it impossible to identify leaks or verify edge. Professional bettors track every bet meticulously.
‚ùå Overconfidence in Hot Streaks: Increasing stakes during winning streaks. Variance works both ways; maintain consistent unit sizing.
